---
title: "Correlation Coefficient: Pearson R between two series"
description: "Pearson Correlation Coefficient measures the linear relationship between two price series. Outputs Correlation R, R-Squared, Variance, and Covariance."
---

Created by Karl Pearson, the Correlation Coefficient quantifies the degree of linear relationship between two historical price series over a rolling lookback window. A value of +1 means perfect positive correlation, −1 means perfect inverse correlation, and 0 means no linear relationship. R-Squared, Variance, and Covariance are also computed and returned alongside the correlation value, making this useful for portfolio analysis, pair trading, and factor research.

## Parameters

<ParamField path="quotesB" type="IReadOnlyList<TQuote>" required>
  Historical quotes for the second series (B). Must have at least the same matching date elements as `quotesA`. Mismatched histories will throw an `InvalidQuotesException`.
</ParamField>

<ParamField path="lookbackPeriods" type="int" required>
  Number of periods (`N`) in the rolling lookback window. Must be greater than 0. A larger period is recommended for a statistically meaningful sample size.
</ParamField>

### Minimum history

You must supply at least `N` periods for both quote series. Both series must share consistent date alignment.

## Result fields

<ResponseField name="Timestamp" type="DateTime" required>
  Date from the evaluated quote.
</ResponseField>

<ResponseField name="VarianceA" type="double">
  Variance of series A over the lookback window.
</ResponseField>

<ResponseField name="VarianceB" type="double">
  Variance of series B over the lookback window.
</ResponseField>

<ResponseField name="Covariance" type="double">
  Covariance of A and B over the lookback window.
</ResponseField>

<ResponseField name="Correlation" type="double">
  Pearson Correlation Coefficient (`R`). Returns `null` for the first `N-1` periods.
</ResponseField>

<ResponseField name="RSquared" type="double">
  R-Squared (R²), also known as the Coefficient of Determination. Computed as the square of `Correlation` using a simple linear regression model.
</ResponseField>

## Usage

```csharp
// batch series
IReadOnlyList<CorrResult> results =
    quotesA.ToCorrelation(quotesB, lookbackPeriods);
```

## Streaming

Streaming is not supported for Correlation. This indicator requires two synchronized quote series, which cannot be expressed in the single-series streaming model. Use the batch implementation with periodic recalculation instead.

## Chaining

Correlation can be generated from any chain-enabled indicator or method. Both series must contain the same number of elements when chaining:

```csharp
var results = quotesA
    .Use(CandlePart.HL2)
    .ToCorrelation(quotesMarket.Use(CandlePart.HL2), lookbackPeriods);
```

<Warning>
  Both `quotesA` and `quotesB` arguments must contain the same number of elements and be the results of a chainable indicator or `.Use()` method.
</Warning>

Results can be further processed on `Correlation`:

```csharp
var results = quotesA
    .ToCorrelation(quotesB, lookbackPeriods)
    .ToSlope(slopePeriods);
```

See [chaining indicators](/guide/batch#chaining-indicators) for more.

## Interpretation

| R value | Relationship |
|---------|-------------|
| `R = +1.0` | Perfect positive correlation |
| `R > +0.7` | Strong positive correlation |
| `R ≈ 0` | No linear relationship |
| `R < -0.7` | Strong inverse correlation |
| `R = -1.0` | Perfect inverse correlation |

R-Squared tells you what fraction of the variance in one series is explained by the other. An R² of 0.80 means 80% of price movements in series A are linearly explained by series B.

<Note>
  Correlation only captures linear relationships. Two series can be strongly related in a nonlinear way while showing a low Pearson R.
</Note>
